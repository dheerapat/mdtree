# Dengue Fever  

## Definition / Overview  
- Dengue fever is a mosquito-transmitted viral illness caused by any of the four distinct serotypes (DENV-1 to DENV-4) of single-stranded RNA viruses in the genus *Flavivirus*. It is the leading arthropod-borne viral disease globally, often termed "breakbone fever" or "7-day fever" due to its symptoms of severe muscle and joint pain, high fever, and its typical duration.  

## Epidemiology  
- Fastest-spreading mosquito-borne viral disease worldwide, with over 100 million cases annually and 20,000–25,000 deaths, primarily among children.  
- Endemic in >100 countries, with epidemic and hyperendemic patterns.  
- Transmission cycles include:  
  - Nonhuman primate to nonhuman primate via mosquitoes.  
  - Human to human via mosquitoes (primarily urban environments).  
- Vectors: *Aedes aegypti* and *Aedes albopictus* (female mosquitoes).  
- Modes of transmission: Perinatal, blood transfusions, breast milk, organ transplantation.  
- Mean age of patients increased from 27.2 (1990–2010) to 34 (post-2010).  

## Etiology / Causes  
- Caused by four serotypes of *Flavivirus* (DENV-1 to DENV-4).  
- Infection with one serotype confers lifelong immunity to that serotype but not others.  

## Pathophysiology  
- Virions (50-nm) contain 3 structural and 7 nonstructural proteins, a lipid envelope, and capped positive-sense ssRNA.  
- 75% of infections are asymptomatic. Spectrum ranges from self-limiting fever to severe hemorrhage/shock.  
- Incubation: 4–7 days; symptoms last 3–10 days.  
- Pathogenesis involves skin macrophages/dendritic cells as initial targets, followed by lymphatic dissemination and viremia (24–48 hours pre-symptoms).  
- Severe dengue (0.5–5% of cases) linked to:  
  - Second infection with a different serotype.  
  - Immune response and viral factors.  
- Microvascular permeability increases as viral titers decline.  

## Clinical Presentation  
### Phases:  
1. **Febrile Phase (2–7 days):**  
   - Sudden high-grade fever (≈40°C), facial flushing, skin erythema (blanchable macular rash), myalgias, arthralgias, headache, sore throat, conjunctival injection, anorexia, nausea, vomiting.  
   - Saddleback/biphasic fever in ≈6% of cases (common in DHF/severe dengue).  

2. **Critical Phase (Days 3–7):**  
   - Defervescence (temperature ↓ to 37.5–38°C or lower).  
   - Capillary permeability increased, lasting 1–2 days.  
   - Preceded by rapid platelet drop, elevated hematocrit, leukopenia.  
   - Warning signs: Abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fluid accumulation (ascites/pleural effusion), mucosal bleeding, lethargy, hepatomegaly, elevated hematocrit, thrombocytopenia.  

3. **Recovery Phase (2–3 days):**  
   - Fluid reabsorption, bradycardia.  
   - **Expanded dengue syndrome:** Atypical manifestations (neurological [seizures, encephalitis], hepatic [hepatitis, failure], renal [AKI], cardiac [myocarditis], respiratory [ARDS], hematologic [HUS]).  

## Evaluation / Diagnosis  
- **Probable Dengue:**  
  - Travel to/history of endemic area + fever + ≥2 of: nausea/vomiting, rash, myalgias/arthralgias, positive tourniquet test, leukopenia.  
- **Warning Signs:** Abdominal pain, persistent vomiting, fluid accumulation, mucosal bleeding, lethargy, hepatomegaly >2 cm, elevated hematocrit, thrombocytopenia.  
- **Severe Dengue:** Dengue fever with severe plasma leakage, hemorrhage, impaired consciousness, myocardial/pulmonary/organ dysfunction (e.g., transaminitis >1000 IU/L).  
- **Diagnostic Tests:**  
  - Laboratory: Thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, elevated AST.  
  - Antigen detection: NS1 ELISA, PCR, viral culture.  
  - Serology: IgM/IgG (detectable after several days; cross-reactivity with other *Flaviviruses*).  

## Differential Diagnosis  
- Measles, influenza, Zika virus, West Nile infection, chikungunya, malaria, yellow fever.  
- Detailed travel/exposure history and lab testing essential.  

## Management / Treatment  
- **Outpatient (no warning signs):**  
  - Acetaminophen (avoid NSAIDs/aspirin), oral fluid repletion.  
  - Patient education on warning signs.  
- **Hospitalization (warning signs/severe dengue/risk factors):**  
  - IV crystalloids (adjust based on response).  
  - Colloids if shock unresponsive to crystalloids.  
  - Blood transfusion for severe/uncontrolled bleeding.  
  - Platelet transfusion if platelets <20,000/µL and high bleeding risk.  
- **No antiviral agents recommended.**  

## Complications  
- Liver injury, cardiomyopathy, pneumonia, orchitis/oophoritis, seizures, encephalopathy, encephalitis.  

## Prognosis  
- Untreated severe dengue mortality: 10–20%.  
- With supportive care: Mortality ↓ to ≈1%.  

## Prevention / Patient Education  
- **Mosquito avoidance:** Bed nets (daytime use), insecticide-treated materials, DEET/IR3535/icaridin repellents, long sleeves/pants.  
- **Biological control:** *Poecilia reticulata* fish, predatory copepods, *Wolbachia*-infected mosquitoes.  
- **Chemical control:** Larvicides, organophosphorus compounds (fenitrothion, malathion), pyrethroids (bioresmethrin, cypermethrin).  
- **Environmental measures:** Eliminate breeding sites, cover water storage.  
- **Vaccination:** CYD-TDV (live recombinant tetravalent vaccine) approved in 20 endemic countries.  

## Interprofessional Considerations  
- Multidisciplinary team: Infectious disease specialists, CDC consultants, emergency clinicians, internists, primary care providers, nurses.  
- Roles:  
  - Timely diagnosis and supportive care (fluids, transfusions).  
  - Primary care providers educate travelers on mosquito avoidance and preventive measures.  
  - Collaboration with public health authorities for outbreak management.
